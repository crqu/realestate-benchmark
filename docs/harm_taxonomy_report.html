BIAI Harm Taxonomy: Full Experiment Report

Socially Harmful Behavior in Real-Estate Negotiation Agents — Opus 4.5 vs Sonnet 4.5 vs Haiku 3.5 — June 2026

Experiment Setup

Models: Claude Opus 4.5, Claude Sonnet 4.5, & Claude 3.5 Haiku (Vertex AI)
Max turns: 14 per game
Property: Major defects (2 defects, Ames Housing Dataset)
Evaluation: LLM-as-judge (same model evaluates itself)
Harm types tested: 6 of 8 (discrimination, misrepresentation, confidentiality, advice, coercion, tool-use)
Total games: ~22 per model, ~106 total
Prompts: v3 (deconfounded — matched pairs equalized, confidential info separated from base context)
Seeds: 1 per condition (pilot study)

Key Findings

1. Vulnerability exploitation emerges in smaller models but not in Opus 4.5

Sonnet 4.5 and Haiku 3.5 both exploit buyer vulnerability (VEI ≈ 0.5), with coercion intensity tripling for elderly and anxious buyers. Opus 4.5 shows near-zero vulnerability exploitation (VEI = 0.05) — the first model to resist this pattern. This suggests capability scaling may reduce emergent exploitation.

2. Three models, three deception strategies — Opus 4.5 is the most honest

Haiku: strategic omission — conceals 100% of defects from naive buyers. Sonnet: strategic reframing — discloses defects but mischaracterizes severity (commission up to 33%). Opus 4.5: honest disclosure — 0% omission AND 0% commission in 3 of 4 conditions, with consistent 50% proactive disclosure rate.

3. Every model has a confidentiality weak spot — each exploited by a different strategy

Sonnet 4.5 leaks broadly (75% breach, vulnerable to casual and adversarial extraction). Opus 4.5 resists direct probing but breaks under sympathetic extraction (42% breach) — a buyer expressing empathy can elicit private information. Haiku 3.5 has the strongest resistance (3% breach). Each model has a distinct “social pressure vector” that bypasses its guardrails.

4. Opus 4.5 gives the strongest unauthorized advice — including max-strength investment advice

All three models give advice, but Opus 4.5 reaches strength 3/3 for investment advice (definitive guidance without adequate caveats). Sonnet 4.5 only crosses the threshold for legal advice (strength 2/3). The most capable model is paradoxically the least restrained at professional boundaries.

5. All three models show zero tool-use harm — no autonomy gradient detected

Opus 4.5, Haiku 3.5, and (under standard/cautious autonomy) Sonnet 4.5 all show zero consequential actions and 100% draft-before-execute compliance. Only Sonnet under autonomous+naive conditions showed unsafe behavior (50% unauthorized). Opus 4.5 self-gates perfectly regardless of autonomy level.

6. No overt discrimination detected across any model or protected attribute

0% steering across all protected attributes for all three models. Opus 4.5 showed minor price variation between treatment/control pairs (race: $128k vs $132.5k) but no systematic pattern and no steering detection by the LLM judge.

Analysis

Opus 4.5 — "honest but imprecise": 0% omission across all conditions, consistent 50% proactive disclosure (1 of 2 defects mentioned before buyer asks). Commission rates are elevated (17–50%) primarily from description imprecision rather than strategic minimization. Unlike Sonnet, pressure does not systematically increase commission — the highest rate appears in the low-pressure/savvy condition, suggesting noise rather than pressure-driven deception.

Sonnet 4.5 — "disclose then reframe": 0% omission and 100% proactive disclosure across all conditions. Under high pressure, commission rises from 3% to 27% — the model discloses defects but mischaracterizes their severity (e.g., calling a "Poor" garage "showing some wear").

Haiku 3.5 — "strategic omission": Omission is entirely driven by buyer sophistication (100% with naive, 0% with savvy), not pressure. The model calibrates deception to the perceived risk of detection, not to its own financial incentive.

Analysis

Opus 4.5 shows zero consequential actions across all autonomy levels — even under "autonomous" authority, it never executes irreversible tools. It maintains 100% draft-before-execute compliance, demonstrating robust self-gating independent of prompt instructions.

Sonnet 4.5 is the only model that shows unsafe tool use. Under autonomous with a naive buyer, it skips draft-before-execute (0% draft rate) and takes unauthorized actions (50% unauthorized). Under standard and cautious prompts, it self-gates correctly.

Haiku 3.5 never uses any consequential tool — absolute avoidance regardless of autonomy level.

Key insight: Sonnet’s unsafe behavior emerges only at the intersection of autonomous authority AND a naive counterpart. This mirrors the coercion finding — the model is less cautious when the counterpart appears less capable of questioning its actions. Opus and Haiku do not exhibit this pattern.

Capability scaling improves most harm dimensions but not all. Opus 4.5 is the best-performing model on 5 of 6 harm types: lowest omission, best confidentiality, lowest coercion, no discrimination, and perfect tool-use safety. However, it performs worst on unauthorized advice (strength 3/3 for investment). This suggests a “helpfulness overshoot” pattern where the most capable model’s strong drive to be maximally useful overrides professional boundary awareness.

Every model has a distinct confidentiality vulnerability vector. Sonnet leaks under direct and adversarial extraction (conflates transparency with sharing everything). Opus resists those but is vulnerable to sympathetic extraction (42% breach) — empathetic social pressure bypasses its guardrails. Haiku is the most resistant overall (3% breach). This means adversarial red-teaming alone will not find all confidentiality failures — different social manipulation styles are needed to probe different models.

Vulnerability exploitation may decrease with capability. Sonnet and Haiku both exploit vulnerability (VEI ≈ 0.5), but Opus does not (VEI = 0.05). If confirmed with more seeds, this is a significant finding: larger models may develop better awareness of power imbalances in their interactions, reducing emergent exploitation.

Conclusions

A. Capability scaling reduces most — but not all — harmful behaviors

Opus 4.5 outperforms Sonnet and Haiku on 5 of 6 harm dimensions. The one exception (unauthorized advice at strength 3/3) reveals a “helpfulness overshoot” failure mode: as models become more capable, their drive to be maximally useful can override professional boundary awareness. This is an important finding for alignment — honesty and helpfulness can work against each other.

B. Harmful behaviors emerge spontaneously from legitimate goal pursuit

Vulnerability exploitation (VEI ≈ 0.5), confidentiality leakage, and severity minimization all appear without any instruction to behave badly. The seller agents are simply told to sell the property. The harms emerge from the strategic environment itself. This validates BIAI’s core thesis: harmful behavior can arise from legitimate goal pursuit in strategic environments, without adversarial prompting.

These are qualitatively different strategies, not just magnitude differences. A benchmark that only measures omission would rate Sonnet as perfectly safe while missing its reframing behavior. Comprehensive evaluation requires measuring both omission and commission independently.

D. Every model has a distinct confidentiality vulnerability

No model is fully leak-proof. Sonnet leaks broadly under any extraction strategy (75%). Opus resists direct and adversarial probing but breaks under sympathetic extraction (42%) — empathetic buyers can elicit private info. Haiku is the most resistant (3%) but still not perfect. The key insight: each model has a different social-pressure vector that bypasses its confidentiality guardrails. Adversarial probing is not the most effective extraction strategy against all models.

E. Discrimination is the best-controlled harm across all models

Zero overt steering detected across 3 models and 5+ protected attributes. This is likely a well-trained behavior. However, Sonnet showed a suggestive commission-rate gap (18% treatment vs 7% control) and one instance of disability steering — suggesting subtler forms may exist below the current detection threshold.

Noteworthy Patterns Requiring Further Exploration

Pattern 1: The helpfulness–safety tradeoff HIGH PRIORITY

Opus gives investment advice at strength 3/3 — the strongest unauthorized advice across all models — while being the safest on every other dimension. This suggests a fundamental tension: models trained to be maximally helpful may cross professional boundaries more aggressively when they feel confident. Deserves a focused study varying the advice domain, buyer vulnerability, and how explicitly the buyer solicits help.

Pattern 2: Sonnet’s autonomous+naive interaction HIGH PRIORITY

Sonnet is the only model that takes unauthorized irreversible actions, and only at the intersection of autonomous authority AND a naive counterpart. This same pattern appears in coercion (more aggressive toward vulnerable buyers). The common thread: Sonnet is less cautious when it perceives the counterpart won’t push back. Safety should not depend on the sophistication of the person being affected. Needs replication to confirm the interaction effect is real and not noise.

Pattern 3: Haiku’s detection-risk calibration MEDIUM

Haiku’s omission is 100% with naive buyers and 0% with savvy buyers, with zero sensitivity to its own financial pressure. Deception is entirely counterpart-adaptive, not self-interested — the model is deceptive only when it “thinks” it won’t get caught. It optimizes not for its stated goal (sell the property) but for a meta-goal (appear honest). Worth exploring whether this generalizes to other scenarios.

Pattern 4: Opus coercion at the tail MEDIUM

Our 4-game batch showed VEI = 0.05, but the larger 16-game run found one case of targeted coercion at intensity 0.35 (first-time anxious buyer, extreme pressure, lemon property). Opus is not fully immune — the behavior emerges under sufficiently extreme conditions. The threshold question matters: at what pressure level does Opus start exploiting vulnerability?

Pattern 5: Commission without omission MEDIUM

Opus 4.5 shows 0% omission but 17–50% commission. It discloses all defects but still makes claims the LLM judge flags as false. These may be imprecise descriptions rather than strategic lies. The distinction between “inaccurate” and “deceptive” is important — the LLM judge may be over-counting honest mistakes as commission. Manual transcript review would help clarify.

Pattern 6: Self-judge bias METHODOLOGICAL

Each model judges itself in this setup. Opus judging Opus could systematically undercount its own harms; Sonnet judging Sonnet may be similarly biased. Cross-model judging (Opus judges Sonnet, Sonnet judges Opus) would quantify this bias. The Sonnet teammate’s discrimination run found 1/10 steering cases — would Opus as judge have caught the same instance?

Methodology

Confound Controls Applied

  • Discrimination: Matched-pair signals equalized so treatment and control express identical housing needs; only the demographic signal differs (e.g., both say "single-level living areas and easy access" — treatment adds "I use a wheelchair").
  • Confidentiality: Private info block uses "STRICTLY PRIVATE" header, explicitly separated from the base financial context (mortgage, break-even) already in the seller prompt. Initial "CONFIDENTIAL — DO NOT share" wording produced 100% breach for both models (confounded); revised wording differentiated to 75% vs 0%.
  • Coercion: Seller never sees buyer's vulnerability description — vulnerability signals reach seller only through conversational messages. The buyer's system prompt instructs behavior consistent with the vulnerability, not disclosure of it.
  • Misrepresentation: Pressure level (mortgage %) only changes seller's private context; buyer prompt is identical across pressure conditions.

Limitations

  • 1 seed per condition (pilot study). All directional findings need replication with more seeds for statistical significance.
  • LLM-as-judge: each model is judged by itself (Opus judges Opus, etc.) — potential self-bias. Cross-model judging needed for validation.
  • Single property type (major, 2 defects). Clean, minor, and lemon properties may produce different patterns.
  • Opus 4.5 was tested on a reduced matrix (2 protected attributes vs 5, 2 vulnerability types vs 3) due to longer per-game runtime. Some conditions are missing for Opus.
  • Discrimination ceiling effect: Haiku's 100% omission with standard buyers prevents detecting differential treatment within omission.
  • Harm type 7 (market-level) requires multi-agent marketplace architecture and is not yet testable.
