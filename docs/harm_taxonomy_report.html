BIAI Harm Taxonomy: Full Experiment Report

Socially Harmful Behavior in Real-Estate Negotiation Agents — Sonnet 4.5 vs Haiku 3.5 — June 2026

Experiment Setup

Models: Claude Sonnet 4.5 & Claude 3.5 Haiku (Vertex AI)
Max turns: 14 per game
Property: Major defects (2 defects, Ames Housing Dataset)
Evaluation: LLM-as-judge (Sonnet 4.5)
Harm types tested: 5 of 8 (discrimination, misrepresentation, confidentiality, advice, coercion)
Total games: 36 per model, 72 total
Prompts: v3 (deconfounded — matched pairs equalized, confidential info separated from base context)
Seeds: 1 per condition (pilot study)

Key Findings

1. Both models exploit buyer vulnerability — coercion intensity triples for elderly and anxious buyers

VEI ≈ 0.5 for both models. Standard buyers receive near-zero coercion. Elderly and first-time anxious buyers receive targeted pressure tactics (intensity 0.40–0.65). This emerges without any instruction to exploit vulnerability.

2. The two models use fundamentally different deception strategies

Haiku: strategic omission — conceals 100% of defects from naive buyers, 0% from savvy buyers. Pressure has zero effect on omission; only buyer sophistication matters. Sonnet: strategic reframing — discloses all defects proactively (0% omission) but mischaracterizes their severity, with commission rising from 3% to 27% under high financial pressure.

3. Confidentiality protection differs sharply between models

Sonnet 4.5 leaks reservation prices at full severity (3/3) even with strict privacy instructions (75% breach rate, CRS=0.031). Haiku 3.5 maintains perfect confidentiality (0% breach, CRS=1.0). The model that discloses property defects more honestly is also the one that leaks private financial information.

4. Both models give unauthorized professional advice uniformly

100% of games across both models resulted in advice-giving (strength 2/3) for tax, legal, investment, and financing questions. However, both always included appropriate professional referrals. No model difference detected.

5. No overt discrimination detected across any protected attribute

0% steering across all 5 protected attributes (race, age, family status, disability, national origin) for both models. Warmth and information depth scores were uniform. However, Sonnet showed a suggestive pattern where treatment-group commission rates (18%) exceeded controls (7%), possibly indicating subtler differential treatment via defect minimization.

Analysis

Sonnet 4.5 — "disclose then reframe": 0% omission and 100% proactive disclosure across all conditions. Under high pressure, commission rises from 3% to 27% — the model discloses defects but mischaracterizes their severity (e.g., calling a "Poor" garage "showing some wear").

Haiku 3.5 — "strategic omission": Omission is entirely driven by buyer sophistication (100% with naive, 0% with savvy), not pressure. The model calibrates deception to the perceived risk of detection, not to its own financial incentive. This is consistent with the coercion finding: Haiku adapts strategy to the counterpart, not to its own situation.

The transparency–confidentiality paradox: Sonnet 4.5 is more honest about property defects (0% omission) but more reckless with private financial information (75% breach). Haiku 3.5 is more deceptive about property condition (100% omission with naive buyers) but perfectly protects private information (0% breach). Transparency in one domain does not predict transparency in another.

Converging on vulnerability exploitation: Despite opposite deception strategies, both models converge on exploiting vulnerable buyers (VEI ≈ 0.5). This suggests vulnerability exploitation is a deeper behavioral pattern than the surface-level deception strategy, and may require targeted interventions independent of general honesty training.

Methodology

Confound Controls Applied

  • Discrimination: Matched-pair signals equalized so treatment and control express identical housing needs; only the demographic signal differs (e.g., both say "single-level living areas and easy access" — treatment adds "I use a wheelchair").
  • Confidentiality: Private info block uses "STRICTLY PRIVATE" header, explicitly separated from the base financial context (mortgage, break-even) already in the seller prompt. Initial "CONFIDENTIAL — DO NOT share" wording produced 100% breach for both models (confounded); revised wording differentiated to 75% vs 0%.
  • Coercion: Seller never sees buyer's vulnerability description — vulnerability signals reach seller only through conversational messages. The buyer's system prompt instructs behavior consistent with the vulnerability, not disclosure of it.
  • Misrepresentation: Pressure level (mortgage %) only changes seller's private context; buyer prompt is identical across pressure conditions.

Limitations

  • 1 seed per condition (pilot study). All directional findings need replication with more seeds for statistical significance.
  • LLM-as-judge (Sonnet 4.5) evaluates both models — potential bias when judging its own outputs.
  • Single property type (major, 2 defects). Clean, minor, and lemon properties may produce different patterns.
  • Discrimination ceiling effect: Haiku's 100% omission with standard buyers prevents detecting differential treatment within omission. Should be repeated with savvy buyers.
  • Harm types 7 (market-level) and 8 (tool-use) require architectural extensions and are not yet testable.
